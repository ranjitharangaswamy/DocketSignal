Design thinking behind the theme dashboard

DocketSignal codes public Reddit legal-AI discourse into eight themes. I did not want every theme to look like the same excerpt card. Each one needed a layout that matched how people were talking about that issue.

Open the live showcase Jump to evidence panel

01 · The problem

One chart cannot carry eight different arguments

Frequency bars answer an important question: where does discourse concentrate? They do not show what it feels like to read adoption pushback, a vendor comparison thread, or a privilege-log win story. Those threads sound different from each other. Some read like warnings. Some read like workshop complaints, policy memos, workflow handoffs, role anxiety, product screenshots, emotional review wins, or billing math.

A generic quote card would flatten all of that texture. It would also make the evidence easier to skim past because every panel would feel interchangeable. For this portfolio piece, I wanted the interface to show qualitative sensitivity. The visualization should respect what the code is about, along with how often it appears.

02 · Design insight

Match metaphor to discourse type and theme label

I treated each theme as a small design brief. Before picking a layout, I asked: if a researcher pinned this thread on a wall during synthesis, what artifact would they reach for? Accuracy threads read like citations to warn a colleague. Adoption friction belongs on a sticky-note culture map. Ethics posts resemble governance memos. Efficiency stories chain like workflow steps. Job-change anxiety attaches to roles more than abstract bars. Tool threads invite side-by-side vendor comparison. eDiscovery posts carry sentiment weight even when the topic is technical. Cost threads land naturally on a ledger.

Different visual forms are part of the analysis. When the form changes with the argument, readers slow down and connect the excerpt to its context.

03 · Cross-discipline stance

Bringing legal reasoning into UX research

This project sits between two parts of my background. From law, I brought attention to evidentiary traceability, professional risk, citation anxiety, confidentiality, billing incentives, and governance. From UX research, I brought coding, synthesis, stakeholder framing, and the question of how to make a messy body of user discourse legible without flattening it.

DocketSignal treats public Reddit threads as early user-research signals for legal-tech product decisions. I read the corpus as a set of professional concerns that shape adoption: whether a lawyer can trust a citation, whether a firm can justify cost, whether a junior worker feels displaced, whether a tool fits review workflow, and whether governance is strong enough for client-facing work.

That is also why the interface keeps source links, subreddit context, scores, and short excerpts visible. Legal training pushes me to ask what supports a claim. UX research pushes me to ask who needs the claim, what decision it informs, and how quickly someone can inspect the evidence. The project is strongest where those habits overlap.

04 · System choices

What stays consistent while the metaphors change

  • Warm legal palette Cream paper, oxblood accents, and antique gold. I wanted the page to feel scholarly and approachable, with less of the cold “legal tech SaaS blue” default.
  • One frequency layer for ranking The dot-and-bar chart handles comparison across themes; evidence panels handle depth within a theme. Separation keeps hierarchy clear.
  • Traceable excerpts Every layout preserves subreddit, score, and source link. The visual treatment should keep the research trail visible.
  • Static-first implementation Layout logic lives in theme-evidence.js with shared CSS tokens. The page stays portable for GitHub Pages portfolio hosting.
  • Responsive containment Sticky boards stack in narrow panels; comparison and feelings layouts collapse gracefully on small viewports.

05 · Process

From pipeline output to portfolio narrative

The analysis pipeline produces counts, excerpts, and memos. The showcase layer adds three reading modes: a scroll-driven story for the research question, an interactive frequency chart for prioritization, and the theme evidence panel for qualitative depth. Design thinking sits beside “Evidence” in the nav because that panel is where the visualization argument lives. It shows how the coding scheme handles different kinds of discourse.

If I extended this project, I would user-test whether distinct metaphors improve recall of theme definitions among legal researchers. I would also test whether a “simple mode” toggle helps first-time viewers who prefer uniform cards. For portfolio purposes, the current version makes the design decision visible in one click per theme.
